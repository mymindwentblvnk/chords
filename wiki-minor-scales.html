Minor Scales and Keys

The Other Essential Pattern

Minor Scales - The Other Essential Pattern

Just like major scales have a specific pattern, minor scales have their own pattern that creates a darker, more emotional sound.

W - H - W - W - H - W - W
Whole - Half - Whole - Whole - Half - Whole - Whole
💡 Notice the Difference:
Major: W-W-H-W-W-W-H
Minor: W-H-W-W-H-W-W

The pattern starts differently, creating a darker sound!

Example: A Natural Minor Scale

Starting from A and applying W-H-W-W-H-W-W:

  1. Start: A
  2. W (Whole): A → B (skip A#)
  3. H (Half): B → C (no key to skip)
  4. W (Whole): C → D (skip C#)
  5. W (Whole): D → E (skip D#)
  6. H (Half): E → F (no key to skip)
  7. W (Whole): F → G (skip F#)
  8. W (Whole): G → A (skip G#)
A Natural Minor Scale: A - B - C - D - E - F - G - A
✓ All white keys! Perfect for beginners learning minor!

Relative Major and Minor

Here's something cool: every major scale has a "relative minor" that uses the exact same notes, just starting from a different position!

C Major and A Minor - Same Notes!

C Major scale: C - D - E - F - G - A - B - C
A Minor scale: A - B - C - D - E - F - G - A

Same 7 notes (all white keys), different starting point!
C major sounds bright, A minor sounds dark.

Natural Chords in Minor Keys

Just like major keys, every minor key has exactly 7 natural chords built from the scale. The pattern is different from major:

minor - diminished - Major - minor - minor - Major - Major
i - ii° - III - iv - v - VI - VII
💡 The Minor Pattern Never Changes!
minor - diminished - Major - minor - minor - Major - Major
i - ii° - III - iv - v - VI - VII

This pattern is the same in EVERY minor key. A minor, E minor, F# minor - they all follow this exact pattern!

Example: Natural Chords in A Minor

Using the skip-pick-skip-pick method from the A minor scale:

Position Roman Numeral Quality Chord Name Notes
1st i minor Am A - C - E
2nd ii° diminished Bdim B - D - F
3rd III Major C C - E - G
4th iv minor Dm D - F - A
5th v minor Em E - G - B
6th VI Major F F - A - C
7th VII Major G G - B - D
🎹 Quick Check:
• If the Roman numeral matches the minor pattern above = Natural chord
• If it doesn't match (like V major instead of v minor) = Borrowed from harmonic minor
Common Exception: The V Chord

In pure natural minor, the V chord is minor (v). However, most music uses a Major V instead because it creates stronger resolution back to i.

Example in A minor:
• Natural: Em (E - G - B)
• Common: E major (E - G# - B)

The G# comes from A harmonic minor, which raises the 7th note to create a leading tone. This is so common that many musicians consider it standard practice!

When Do I Use Minor?

If you see a progression starting with lowercase "i" (like i - VI - III - VII), you're in a minor key. Use the W-H-W-W-H-W-W pattern!
